## CARE

### Circuit Overview

The CARE circuit is organized around the medial preoptic area (MPOA) of the hypothalamus as the central integrative hub. Galanin-expressing neurons (MPOAGal) in the MPOA receive multimodal infant sensory inputs (olfactory, auditory, somatosensory) and coordinate parental responses via two major output routes: (1) inhibition of an antisocial defensive circuit (via suppression of amygdala-mediated avoidance), and (2) excitation of a prosocial approach/reward circuit (via projections to VTA dopamine neurons).

Hormonal priming is essential for the onset of maternal behavior. Estrogen, progesterone, prolactin, and oxytocin converge on the MPOA to signal reproductive state changes. Prolactin receptor activation in MPOA neurons is necessary for postpartum nursing behavior; acute deletion abolishes maternal care. Oxytocin from the paraventricular nucleus (PVN) modulates multiple targets: it enhances auditory cortical responses to pup calls (left auditory cortex), dampens amygdala threat responses during social interaction, and coordinates with dopamine in the nucleus accumbens to promote social reward and bonding.

The VTA-NAc dopamine pathway provides the motivational substrate for parental care. Infant cues (smiling faces, cries) activate VTA/substantia nigra regions, striatum, and prefrontal cortex in human parents, with graded responses dependent on infant affect (happy > neutral > sad). The BNST serves as an interface between the MPOA and downstream reward/anxiety circuits, integrating hormonal signals with infant-related sensory inputs.

Epigenetic mechanisms transmit care quality across generations: high maternal licking/grooming in rats alters DNA methylation at the glucocorticoid receptor promoter in offspring hippocampus, programming lifelong HPA axis sensitivity. Similarly, estrogen receptor expression in the MPOA is epigenetically regulated by received maternal care, influencing future maternal behavior.

## PANIC/GRIEF

### Circuit Overview

The PANIC/GRIEF circuit mediates separation distress, social loss, and the psychic pain of broken social bonds. Panksepp identified this as one of seven primary-process emotional systems, centered on the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), bed nucleus of the stria terminalis (BNST), preoptic area, dorsomedial thalamus, and periaqueductal gray (PAG).

The dorsal ACC (dACC) is the critical hub for "social pain," activated during both physical pain and social exclusion. The Eisenberger et al. (2003) cyberball paradigm demonstrated that social exclusion activates dACC and anterior insula in patterns parallel to physical pain processing. The subgenual ACC (sgACC) is specifically linked to yearning during grief. The right ventral prefrontal cortex (RVPFC) regulates social distress by disrupting ACC activity.

Neurochemically, the PANIC/GRIEF system is regulated by three key systems: (1) Endogenous opioids -- morphine reduces and naloxone potentiates separation distress vocalizations, suggesting social bonds are maintained by tonic mu-opioid receptor activation (an "endorphin addiction" model of attachment). (2) Oxytocin -- social contact releases OXT which buffers stress responses; social isolation reduces OXT signaling. (3) CRH/cortisol -- the HPA axis activates during social loss, with elevated CRH driving anxiety and distress behaviors.

In grief, neuroimaging reveals activation of the posterior cingulate, insula, dACC, and medial prefrontal cortex. Critically, complicated (prolonged) grief additionally activates the nucleus accumbens, suggesting that reward-system involvement in attachment may interfere with adaptation to loss. Social rejection activates secondary somatosensory cortex and dorsal posterior insula, regions previously thought specific to physical pain sensation.

The PAG mediates distress vocalizations: dorsolateral PAG drives active distress calls, while ventrolateral PAG mediates passive coping (withdrawal, immobility). Distinct PAG neuron populations regulate social versus distress calls.

## LUST

### Circuit Overview

The LUST circuit governs sexual motivation and consummatory sexual behavior, organized around hypothalamic nuclei as central integrating hubs. The medial preoptic area (MPOA) is the primary center for male sexual behavior across all studied vertebrate species, while the ventromedial hypothalamus (VMH) is critical for female sexual receptivity (lordosis reflex). These regions are rich in sex steroid receptors (estrogen receptor alpha, progesterone receptor, androgen receptor).

In males, dopamine release in the MPOA is both necessary and sufficient for sexual motivation and copulation. Testosterone is aromatized to estradiol locally in the MPOA, and this neuroestrogen rapidly regulates sexual motivation (but not performance) through membrane-initiated signaling. In females, the lordosis circuit involves a VMH→PAG→reticular formation→spinal motoneuron pathway, with estrogen priming of VMH neurons as the critical hormonal gate.

The reward/motivation component involves VTA dopamine projections to the nucleus accumbens, providing the reinforcing properties of sexual behavior. Pair-bond formation in monogamous species (prairie voles) requires concurrent activation of dopamine D2 receptors and oxytocin receptors (females) or vasopressin V1a receptors (males) in the NAc. D1 receptor upregulation after pair bond formation mediates selective aggression toward novel mates (bond maintenance).

The amygdala processes sexually relevant stimuli: men show greater amygdala and hypothalamic activation than women to visual sexual stimuli, even when women report equal subjective arousal. Human neuroimaging meta-analyses identify consistent activation in hypothalamus, anterior cingulate, insula, striatum, thalamus, and orbitofrontal cortex during sexual arousal.

## PLAY

### Circuit Overview

The PLAY circuit governs social play behavior (rough-and-tumble play in mammals), identified by Panksepp as a distinct primary-process emotional system. The neural substrate centers on the parafascicular nucleus of the thalamus as a critical hub, with an executive circuit including thalamic intralaminar nuclei, frontal cortex, and striatum.

Discrete damage to the parafascicular area (PFA) of the thalamus results in robust, long-lasting, and selective impairment of play (73% reduction in pinning) without compromising other sensory-motor processes. The PFA receives direct somatosensory input from the spinal cord and sends excitatory projections to frontal cortex and striatum, forming a somatosensory-integration-to-motor circuit essential for the tactile reciprocity of play.

Neurochemically, play is modulated by four key systems: (1) Endocannabinoids enhance play behavior via CB1 receptors in the basolateral amygdala and nucleus accumbens; anandamide levels increase in amygdala and NAc during play. (2) Endogenous opioids enhance play through mu-opioid receptors in the NAc, mediating the rewarding/pleasurable aspects. (3) Dopamine in the NAc modulates play motivation (not expression): D1 and D2 receptor activation increase play, while DA depletion reduces motivation to engage. (4) Acetylcholine and norepinephrine also modulate play, though less specifically.

Play deprivation during the juvenile period has lasting consequences: it prevents normal dendritic pruning in the medial prefrontal cortex, impairs executive function and social skills, and produces adults with socio-cognitive deficiencies. The 50-kHz ultrasonic vocalizations ("rat laughter") emitted during play and tickling serve as biomarkers of positive affective state, driven by dopaminergic mechanisms in the NAc. Tickling-evoked neural activity in somatosensory cortex (trunk region, deep layers) predicts play-related neural responses.

## SURPRISE

### Circuit Overview

The SURPRISE circuit processes unexpected events, prediction errors, and novel stimuli, enabling rapid behavioral adaptation. It is not a single anatomical circuit but rather a distributed network of prediction-error and novelty-detection mechanisms spanning multiple brain systems.

The locus coeruleus (LC) is the primary noradrenergic hub for surprise signaling. LC neurons fire phasically to unexpected and salient events, broadcasting prediction errors across the cortex through diffuse norepinephrine release. The adaptive gain theory (Aston-Jones & Cohen 2005) proposes two modes: phasic LC activity facilitates exploitation of current task demands, while tonic activity promotes exploration after model failure. The LC acts as a "global model failure system" -- when ongoing events deviate from predictions, the LC fires to promote sensorimotor plasticity and behavioral adjustment.

The dopamine system provides a complementary signed prediction error signal: VTA dopamine neurons encode reward prediction errors (Schultz 1997) -- activated by better-than-expected outcomes, unresponsive to fully predicted rewards, and suppressed by worse-than-expected outcomes. This signal drives reinforcement learning and motivational updating.

The hippocampus serves as a novelty/mismatch detector, comparing incoming sensory information with stored predictions. The hippocampal-VTA loop (Lisman & Grace 2005) enables novelty-dependent memory consolidation: novel events detected by hippocampus trigger dopamine release from VTA back to hippocampus, enhancing LTP and long-term memory formation. The locus coeruleus co-releases dopamine in the hippocampus, further enhancing novelty-driven memory.

The amygdala responds to novel and salient stimuli regardless of valence, particularly to novel human faces. The ventral attention network (temporoparietal junction + ventral frontal cortex, right-lateralized) serves as a "circuit breaker" for the dorsal attention network, reorienting attention to unexpected salient events.

Electrophysiological markers include the mismatch negativity (MMN, 100-250ms) for automatic deviance detection in auditory cortex and the P300 (250-500ms) for higher-order context updating. The anterior cingulate cortex encodes unsigned prediction errors (surprise regardless of sign), driving behavioral adjustment.

### Circuit Diagram (text)

```
UNEXPECTED EVENT / PREDICTION VIOLATION
    │
    ├──► Locus Coeruleus (NE burst)
    │       ├── Phasic mode: task-relevant surprise → exploitation adjustment
    │       ├── Tonic mode: global model failure → exploration switch
    │       ├──► Cortex (diffuse NE): sensory gain enhancement, plasticity
    │       ├──► Hippocampus: DA co-release → memory encoding of novel events
    │       └── Inputs: ACC/OFC (task utility), hippocampus (state PE)
    │
    ├──► VTA DA neurons (signed reward prediction error)
    │       ├── Better than expected → DA burst → positive RPE
    │       ├── As expected → baseline → no learning signal
    │       ├── Worse than expected → DA dip → negative RPE
    │       └──► NAc, PFC, hippocampus
    │
    ├──► Hippocampus (novelty/mismatch detection)
    │       ├── Compare incoming input vs stored representations
    │       ├──► subiculum → NAc → VP → VTA (novelty signal)
    │       └──► VTA DA → hippocampus (enhanced LTP, memory consolidation)
    │
    ├──► Amygdala (salience detection, especially novel humans/faces)
    │       ├── Unsigned salience signal (novel, unexpected regardless of valence)
    │       └── Feeds into ventral attention network
    │
    ├──► dACC (unsigned prediction error / surprise)
    │       ├── Enhanced responses to both large and small unexpected outcomes
    │       └── Drives behavioral adjustment, conflict monitoring
    │
    ├──► Anterior Insula (unsigned surprise, interoceptive prediction error)
    │
    └──► Ventral Attention Network (right TPJ + VFC)
            └── "Circuit breaker": reorients attention from current focus to unexpected event

ELECTROPHYSIOLOGY:
    MMN (100-250ms): automatic deviance detection in auditory cortex
    P300 (250-500ms): context updating, working memory revision
    N400: semantic prediction error
```

## Cross-System Interactions

### CARE <-> PANIC/GRIEF
- CARE circuit activation (OXT, opioids) directly suppresses PANIC/GRIEF circuit
- Maternal contact buffers infant HPA axis (social buffering)
- Loss of caregiver = loss of OXT/opioid supply = PANIC activation
- Early care quality epigenetically programs lifelong PANIC sensitivity (GR methylation)

### CARE <-> LUST
- Shared neural substrates: MPOA governs both parental and sexual behavior (distinct cell populations)
- OXT released during both nursing and mating; facilitates both bonding types
- VTA-NAc DA pathway underlies reward of both infant cues and sexual stimuli
- Pair bonding (LUST→CARE transition): D2→D1 receptor shift in NAc

### PLAY <-> CARE
- Maternal separation disrupts subsequent play development
- Play provides developmental substrate for social competence required for later caregiving
- Shared opioid and DA reward mechanisms

### PLAY <-> PANIC/GRIEF
- Play deprivation and social isolation both activate stress pathways
- Play experience buffers against separation distress
- Opioid system mediates both play reward and separation distress

### SURPRISE <-> All systems
- LC-NE surprise signal modulates all emotion circuits via diffuse projections
- Unexpected social events (separation, novel infant cues) recruit both SURPRISE and emotion-specific circuits
- Prediction error signals gate learning in fear conditioning, reward learning, and social bonding
